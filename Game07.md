Although Black managed to capture the vertical ‘h’, this fact does not mean that Black has gained an advantage.

By the way, how do I count tempos?  
Checkers located on the first row are worth 1 point, those on the second row are worth 2 points, and so on.  
For example, the total tempo number for White's position is 5x1 + 2x2 + 5x3 + 4x4 = 40, and for Black's position it is 4x1 + 5x2 + 5x3 + 2x4 = 37.  
Of course, this is a rough estimate and does not apply to all positions.

What should we pay attention to?  

Black has a significant advantage in development, so it is not in White’s interest to exchange the piece e4 for the piece h5, for example.  
In this case, the piece f5 moves to the h1 square. The white king may be on the h1 square, but the black king can push it off from the h8 square, threatening to capture it.

In addition, if we imagine that after the exchanges only the kings and the simple pieces on a1 and d5 remain, then this endgame is lost for White.  
Black simply gets a second king more quickly and wins.  
From this, we conclude that White should try to get a second king and use tactical chances against Black’s disjointed pieces.
